What the Cardamom Price Has Been Trying to Tell Us

Eleven years. 5,440 auctions. One small green pod whose price has swung nine-fold. Here is what the numbers say about when to sell, when to plant, and where the market really gets made.

01

A price with a temper

Few farm commodities move like cardamom. Since 2014 the auction price has travelled from under ₹500 to nearly ₹4,700 a kilo and back again — a roller-coaster driven by monsoons, floods and the slow churn of planting cycles.

Monthly average auction price, Nov 2014 – Feb 2026. Shaded bands mark the major bull and bear runs the data identifies.
What this means for you

Cardamom is a boom-and-bust crop. The single biggest fortunes — and ruins — were made by those who read the cycle, not the week. The 2019 flood spike to ₹4,675 was followed by a two-thirds collapse. Plan for the swing; never assume today's price is the new normal.

"The market spends years quietly cheap, then re-prices violently in a single season."

What actually moves the price

We let the forecasting models rank every signal by how much it improves a real, out-of-sample price prediction. The verdict is humbling for chartists: weather wins.

What this means for you

Over a season, monsoon rainfall in Idukki is the master variable — it shapes next year's supply more than any price chart. Watch the rains, the El Niño/La Niña cycle, and the rupee. Day-to-day, momentum and how much the auction houses disagree matter most.

04

The planter's paradox

A new cardamom field takes about three years to come into full bearing. So the price that matters isn't today's — it's the one waiting for you at harvest. History is brutally clear about when to put plants in the ground.

05

Where you sell is worth money

On any given day two or three auction houses run their own sales. They are not interchangeable. Comparing each house against the same-day market reveals a stubborn pecking order that has held for years.

Average price achieved by each house relative to the same-day market average. Green houses consistently clear above the market; red ones below.
What this means for you

A market that runs on a weekly clock

Each major house owns a weekday. The "daily price" is really a rotating cast — part of every day-to-day wobble is just which house sold, not the market moving.

A quiet tell: when the houses disagree

When same-day auction prices scatter widely, the next two weeks tend to move more — and lean upward. Disagreement marks demand building under the surface.
